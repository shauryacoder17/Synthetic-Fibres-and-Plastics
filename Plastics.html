Plastics



Plastics are a wide range of synthetic or semi-synthetic materials that use polymers as a main ingredient. In plastics, the arrangement of the individual units may be linear or cross-linked.
1. Cross-linked polymers- These polymers have side chains or cross-links, which connect different polymer chains.
2. Linear polymers- In linear polymers, the monomers are linked linearly to each other.

Depending on their physical properties there are two types of plastics: Thermoplastics and Thermosetting plastics.

a) Thermoplastics:
• Linear and lightly cross-linked polymers form a class of plastics called thermoplastics.
• They are much tougher and they have much better inter laminar strength and resistance to impact.
• Thermoplastics can be melted by heating and thereafter moulded into desired shapes. This is a reversible process.
• They absorb much less moisture with less consequential reduction in elevated temperature mechanical properties.
• PVC(polyvinyl chloride) and LDPE(low-density polyethylene) are examples of thermoplastics.

b) Thermosetting Plastics
• Heavy cross-linking polymers after shaping the plastic form a class of plastics called thermosetting plastics.
• Their tensile strength is high and the melting points of thermosetting plastics are high.
• Unlike thermoplastics, thermosetting plastics cannot be remolded after reheating.
• Thermosets have a brittle structure and lack the ductile qualities of thermoplastics.
• Because of their cross-linked molecular structure, thermosets have more stiffness and superior mechanical properties than thermoplastics.
• Melamine and bakelite are examples of thermosetting plastics.
